Refrigerant Recovery, Recycling & Reclamation: EPA 608 Guide

The three terms every EPA 608 technician must know cold — and how to distinguish them on the proctored exam.

EPA 608 Practice Test › Study Guides › Recovery, Recycling & Reclamation

The EPA 608 exam consistently tests technicians on the distinction between recovery, recycling, and reclamation. These three terms are not interchangeable — each describes a distinct process governed by different regulations, performed by different entities, and producing refrigerant at different purity levels. Confusing them on the proctored exam is among the most common reasons technicians fail the Core section.

The Three Processes — Quick Reference
  • • Recovery — Remove refrigerant from system into approved container. Cannot reuse until processed. Any certified tech.
  • • Recycling — Clean recovered refrigerant with oil separators and filter-dryers. Reuse on-site same/similar equipment. Any certified tech with recycling equipment.
  • • Reclamation — Process to ARI 700 purity standards. Only EPA-certified reclaim facilities. Refrigerant can be resold.

The Three Processes Defined

Process 1

Recovery — Removing Refrigerant from a System

Definition: Recovery is the process of removing refrigerant from a system into an approved, external recovery container. Recovered refrigerant has not been cleaned or processed and cannot be returned to different equipment or sold until it undergoes further processing.

Who performs it: Any EPA 608-certified technician using EPA-certified recovery equipment.

Reuse rules:

  • Can be returned to the same system it came from without additional processing
  • Cannot be used in a different system without recycling first (same owner) or reclaiming (different owner)
  • Cannot be sold or transferred to another party without reclamation
Process 2

Recycling — On-Site Cleaning for Reuse

Definition: Recycling is the cleaning of recovered refrigerant using oil separators, filter-dryers, and non-condensable gas purging to reduce moisture, acid, and oil contamination. It is performed on-site by the technician using recycling equipment.

Who performs it: Any EPA 608-certified technician with EPA-certified recycling equipment.

Reuse rules:

  • Recycled refrigerant can be used in the same or similar type of equipment owned by the same owner
  • Recycled refrigerant cannot be sold to a different owner
  • Recycled refrigerant does not meet ARI 700 purity standards — it is "good enough" for on-site reuse only
Process 3

Reclamation — Certified Processing to Virgin Purity

Definition: Reclamation is the reprocessing of recovered refrigerant to the purity standards of ARI 700 (now called AHRI 700) — equivalent to virgin refrigerant. Reclamation cannot be performed on-site by a field technician; it requires an EPA-certified reclamation facility with laboratory testing equipment.

Who performs it: Only EPA-certified reclamation facilities.

Reuse rules:

  • Reclaimed refrigerant can be resold to any buyer as if it were virgin refrigerant
  • Reclaimed refrigerant can be used in any equipment, regardless of owner
  • Reclaimed refrigerant meets the highest purity standard (ARI 700)

⚠️ Most Common Exam Mistake

Technicians confuse "recycling" with "reclamation." Remember: Recycling is what a technician does in the field with portable equipment — it cleans refrigerant for on-site reuse only. Reclamation requires a certified facility and produces refrigerant that can be resold. If a question involves "resale" or "purity standards," the answer is reclamation.

Recovery Efficiency Requirements

The EPA sets minimum recovery efficiency levels based on equipment type, charge size, and manufacture date. These numbers are heavily tested on the exam — particularly the 90% and 80% thresholds and the November 15, 1993 cutoff date.

Key Number: November 15, 1993

This date separates the 80% and 90% recovery efficiency requirements for Type II and Type III systems. Equipment manufactured before this date requires only 80% recovery efficiency. Equipment manufactured on or after this date requires 90%. The exam uses this date in scenario questions — always identify the manufacture date before selecting your recovery efficiency answer.

Recovery Equipment Requirements

  • EPA-certified recovery equipment required: Technicians must use EPA-certified recovery or recycling equipment. Equipment manufactured after November 15, 1993, must meet EPA certification standards.
  • No disposable cylinders: Recovered refrigerant must go into approved, reusable DOT cylinders — never into disposable (one-time-use) cylinders, which may only be used for virgin refrigerant.
  • Gray cylinder with yellow top: The required color for recovery cylinders containing recovered (potentially mixed or contaminated) refrigerant. This prevents mixing with virgin refrigerant stored in cylinders with standard refrigerant-specific color codes.
  • Never mix refrigerants in recovery cylinders: Different refrigerant types must be recovered into separate cylinders. Mixing makes reclamation impossible and creates hazardous pressure conditions.
  • System-dependent recovery: The option to use the system compressor to assist recovery (system-dependent recovery) is available when the compressor is operational. This is the primary recovery method for small appliances.

Venting Prohibition and Penalties

Section 608 of the Clean Air Act prohibits any person from knowingly venting refrigerants during the service, maintenance, repair, or disposal of refrigeration and air conditioning equipment. This applies to all refrigerants — not just ozone-depleting CFCs and HCFCs, but also non-ozone-depleting HFCs and HFOs.

  • Civil penalties: Up to $44,539 per day per violation for knowing venting
  • Employer liability: Employers are liable for violations by their employees
  • Citizen suit provision: Any person (including competitors) can sue to enforce Section 608 violations
  • De minimis exception: Incidental releases during good faith recovery attempts are not considered violations

Exam Strategy: Memorizing Recovery, Recycling, Reclamation

Use these three memory anchors when answering exam questions:

  1. Resale = Reclamation. Any question involving resale, ownership transfer, or ARI 700 purity → answer is reclamation.
  2. On-site cleaning = Recycling. Any question involving on-site reuse in the same equipment type by the same owner → answer is recycling.
  3. Just removed it = Recovery. Any question about removing refrigerant from a system into a cylinder without specifying further processing → answer is recovery.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between refrigerant recovery, recycling, and reclamation?

Recovery removes refrigerant from a system into a container — no processing, not yet reusable in different equipment. Recycling cleans recovered refrigerant with oil separators and filter-dryers for on-site reuse in the same or similar equipment owned by the same owner. Reclamation processes refrigerant to ARI 700 virgin purity standards at a certified facility — reclaimed refrigerant can be resold and used in any equipment.

What are the required refrigerant recovery efficiency levels?

For high-pressure (Type II) and low-pressure (Type III) systems manufactured after November 15, 1993: 90% efficiency required. For the same systems manufactured before November 15, 1993: 80% required. For small appliances (Type I): 80% if the system compressor works, 90% if the compressor is non-functioning — the manufacture date does not change the small appliance standard.

Who can perform refrigerant reclamation?

Only EPA-certified reclamation facilities. Individual field technicians, regardless of their EPA 608 certification level, cannot perform reclamation on-site. Reclaimed refrigerant must meet ARI 700 (AHRI 700) purity standards verified by lab analysis before it can be resold or used in equipment belonging to a different owner.

What color are refrigerant recovery cylinders?

Gray body with a yellow top. This color combination identifies the cylinder as containing recovered (used, potentially contaminated) refrigerant. Never use disposable cylinders for recovery. Never mix different refrigerant types in the same recovery cylinder — mix contamination makes reclamation impossible.

Can recovered refrigerant be reused without processing?

Only if it is returned to the exact same system it came from. To use recovered refrigerant in any other system (even owned by the same owner), it must first be recycled. To use it in equipment owned by a different person or to sell it, it must be reclaimed to ARI 700 standards by a certified reclamation facility.

Is it legal to vent refrigerant during service?

No. Section 608 of the Clean Air Act prohibits knowingly venting any refrigerant — CFCs, HCFCs, HFCs, and HFOs — during service, maintenance, repair, or disposal of equipment. Civil penalties reach $44,539 per day per violation. The only exception is de minimis releases (unavoidable incidental releases during good faith recovery attempts).
