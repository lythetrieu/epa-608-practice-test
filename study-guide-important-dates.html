Chronological Regulatory Timeline

  • 1987

    The Montreal Protocol is Signed

    This international treaty was signed to protect the stratospheric ozone layer by phasing out the production of ozone-depleting substances (ODS) like CFCs and HCFCs.

  • 1990

    Clean Air Act Amendments

    The United States Congress amended the Clean Air Act, establishing Section 608 which mandates technician certification and recovery of refrigerants.

  • July 1, 1992

    Mandatory Venting Ban

    It became illegal to knowingly vent CFC and HCFC refrigerants into the atmosphere during service, repair, or disposal of HVAC equipment.

  • 1996

    CFC Production Ban

    The production and import of CFC refrigerants (like R-12) was completely banned in the United States under the Montreal Protocol phaseout schedule.

  • Jan 1, 2020

    HCFC-22 Production Ban

    The manufacture and import of virgin R-22 and R-142b was banned. Existing systems must rely on reclaimed or recycled refrigerant.

  • Jan 1, 2025

    AIM Act GWP Restrictions

    New regulations under the AIM Act began restricting the use of high-GWP HFCs (like R-410A) in new residential and light commercial equipment.

Memory Techniques for Regulatory Milestones

Technicians often struggle with the difference between 1987 and 1996. A simple way to remember is that Montreal (1987) was the promise, while the 1996 Ban was the punishment for CFC production.

  • 1992 vs 1995: Venting became illegal first (1992), and technician certification followed shortly after (1995).

AIM Act 2026: What Changes for Technicians

The American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act accelerates the shift from HFCs to low-GWP alternatives. For the 2026 EPA 608 exam, know these facts:

  • R-410A (GWP: 2,088) is being phased out â€” new residential equipment must use lower-GWP alternatives by 2025

Frequently Asked Questions

What date was the venting of refrigerants banned?

Effective July 1, 1992, under Clean Air Act Section 608. This prohibition applies to all regulated refrigerants â€” CFC, HCFC, and HFC. Civil penalties up to $44,539 per day per violation apply. This is one of the most commonly tested dates on the Core exam.

When was R-22 completely phased out?

R-22 production and import was banned January 1, 2020. The earlier milestone was January 1, 2010, when R-22 was banned in new equipment. Today, only reclaimed or recovered R-22 may be used to service existing systems â€” no new R-22 can be produced in or imported into the U.S.

Why is November 15, 1993 important for the exam?

This is the dividing line for recovery equipment certification requirements. Equipment manufactured after November 15, 1993, must meet stricter vacuum and recovery level standards. Exam questions frequently ask you to choose different recovery levels depending on whether equipment was made before or after this date.

When was the AIM Act signed?

The American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act was signed into law in December 2020. EPA published the HFC phasedown rules in 2021, with the first restrictions taking effect in 2022. R-410A was banned in new residential equipment after January 1, 2025.

When did Clean Air Act Section 608 go into effect?

Section 608 was established by the Clean Air Act Amendments of 1990. This is the legal foundation for all EPA 608 certification requirements, venting prohibitions, and recovery standards. The venting ban became effective July 1, 1992 â€” two years after the law passed.

When did CFC production end?

U.S. CFC production and import ended January 1, 1996, under the Montreal Protocol schedule. CFCs include R-11, R-12, R-113, R-114, and R-115. These may still exist in old equipment â€” technicians can service these systems using reclaimed CFCs but cannot purchase new supplies.
