EPA 608 Practice Test: Clean Air Act Section 608 Requirements

Section 608 of the Clean Air Act mandates the protection of stratospheric ozone through strict refrigerant handling regulations. These laws prohibit the intentional venting of ozone-depleting substances and their substitutes while requiring technician certification for service work.

  • Certification Mandate: Any individual who maintains, services, or repairs equipment containing refrigerant must hold a valid Section 608 certification.
  • Prohibition of Venting: The law makes it illegal to knowingly release CFC, HCFC, and HFC refrigerants during maintenance or disposal.
  • Recovery Standards: Technicians must use certified recovery equipment to remove refrigerant to specified vacuum levels before opening systems.

Montreal Protocol (EPA 608 Practice Test Overview)

The Montreal Protocol serves as an international treaty designed to protect the ozone layer by phasing out the production of ozone-depleting substances. Signed in 1987, this agreement has led to the successful elimination of CFCs and the ongoing phase-down of HCFCs and HFCs.

Stratospheric Ozone Depletion and Environmental Impact

Ozone molecules in the stratosphere absorb harmful ultraviolet radiation from the sun, protecting life on Earth. Refrigerants containing chlorine, such as CFCs and HCFCs, reach the upper atmosphere where solar radiation breaks them down.

A single chlorine atom destroys over 100,000 ozone molecules through a catalytic chain reaction. This process creates the ozone hole, leading to increased rates of skin cancer, cataracts, and environmental damage.

Refrigerant Classifications and ASHRAE Safety Groups

ASHRAE Standard 34 classifies refrigerants based on their toxicity and flammability. Toxicity uses letters (A for lower, B for higher), while flammability uses numbers (1 for none, 2 for low, 3 for high).

  • Class A1: Non-flammable and low toxicity (e.g., R-22, R-134a, R-410A).
  • Class A2L: Mildly flammable with low toxicity (e.g., R-32, R-1234yf).
  • Class B1: Non-flammable but higher toxicity (e.g., R-123).

The Three R's: Recover, Recycle, Reclaim

The Core exam tests whether you understand the distinction between three refrigerant handling processes. Confusing them is one of the most common wrong answers on the EPA 608 exam.

Process Definition Where It Happens
Recover Remove refrigerant from a system in any condition without testing or processing Job site / field
Recycle Clean refrigerant for reuse on-site using oil separation and single-pass filtration Field (same job)
Reclaim Reprocess refrigerant to ARI 700 purity standards â€” requires laboratory equipment EPA-certified facility only

Key exam fact: Only Reclaim restores refrigerant to virgin-equivalent purity. Recycled refrigerant cannot be sold or transferred to a different owner.

Refrigerant Cylinder Color Codes & Shipping Standards

The Core section tests cylinder identification because mishandling refrigerant cylinders causes pressure explosions and illegal venting. The EPA mandates specific colors under ASHRAE Standard 34.

Refrigerant Cylinder Color Safety Group
R-22 Green A1
R-410A Pink (Rose) A1
R-134a Light Blue A1
R-404A Orange A1
R-32 Blue A2L (mildly flammable)
Recovery cylinders Gray body / Yellow top Universal (any refrigerant)

Shipping rule: Refrigerant cylinders transported by road must comply with DOT regulations. Cylinders must never be filled beyond 80% of capacity (liquid refrigerant expands with heat). Overfilled cylinders are illegal to transport.
