What Is EPA 608 Certification? (EPA 608 Practice Test Overview)

EPA Section 608 Technician Certification is a federal credential administered by EPA-approved third-party organizations. It was created under Section 608 of the Clean Air Act Amendments of 1990, which gave the EPA authority to regulate the handling of ozone-depleting substances used as refrigerants in stationary equipment.

The certification program exists for one core reason: refrigerants are harmful to the environment when vented intentionally or carelessly. Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs) contain chlorine atoms that destroy stratospheric ozone. Even modern hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) refrigerants like R-410A, while ozone-safe, are potent greenhouse gases with global warming potentials (GWP) hundreds to thousands of times higher than carbon dioxide.

By requiring that all refrigerant handlers pass a standardized exam, the EPA ensures a baseline level of knowledge across the entire HVAC workforce — preventing illegal venting, reducing accidental releases, and maintaining compliance with international treaty obligations under the Montreal Protocol.

Who Needs EPA 608 Certification?

Anyone who maintains, services, repairs, or disposes of equipment that uses refrigerants in stationary applications. This includes residential HVAC technicians, commercial refrigeration service technicians, industrial chiller operators, and anyone who purchases bulk refrigerant. The requirement applies to technicians employed by companies as well as self-employed contractors.

The Four Types of EPA 608 Certification Explained

The EPA 608 exam is divided into a mandatory Core section plus three Type-specific sections. The Core covers universal environmental law, safety, and refrigerant handling fundamentals. The Type sections test knowledge specific to different refrigerant system categories.

Type I — Small Appliances

Covers equipment with 5 lbs or less of refrigerant that was fully manufactured, charged, and hermetically sealed at the factory. Examples include window AC units, household refrigerators, water coolers, and vending machines.

Key refrigerants: R-12, R-134a, R-600a (isobutane)

Core + Type I required

Type II — High-Pressure

Covers high-pressure equipment with normal operating pressures above atmospheric. The most common category — includes residential central AC, split systems, heat pumps, medium-temperature commercial refrigeration, and most light commercial HVAC.

Key refrigerants: R-22, R-410A, R-32, R-454B

Core + Type II required

Type III — Low-Pressure

Covers low-pressure equipment that operates below atmospheric pressure (in vacuum). Primarily large centrifugal chillers found in commercial buildings, hospitals, and industrial facilities. These systems use significantly larger refrigerant charges.

Key refrigerants: R-11, R-113, R-123

Core + Type III required

Universal Certification

Awarded when you pass the Core section plus all three Type sections (I, II, and III) in a single sitting or across multiple exams. Universal certification qualifies you to work on any refrigerant system — the most valuable credential for career advancement and commercial work.

Best for: Commercial techs, service managers, those pursuing Universal HVAC roles

Core + I + II + III

The Core Section: What It Covers

The Core section is the foundation of all EPA 608 certification. It must be passed before any Type certification is awarded. The Core exam contains 25 questions covering:

  • Clean Air Act regulations: Venting prohibition, enforcement authority, required records, reporting obligations
  • Refrigerant chemistry: ODP (Ozone Depletion Potential), GWP (Global Warming Potential), CFC vs HCFC vs HFC vs HFO classifications
  • Recovery requirements: Required recovery efficiencies by equipment type, required equipment certification standards
  • Ozone depletion science: The mechanism of ozone depletion, the Montreal Protocol, phase-down schedules
  • Safety: Refrigerant exposure risks, PPE requirements, first-aid procedures for refrigerant contact

The Core section is consistently rated the most difficult section by test-takers, because it requires memorizing regulatory statutes and chemistry concepts that aren't typically used in daily field work. Use our Core Practice Test to drill these concepts before exam day.

Passing Score and Exam Structure

Each section of the EPA 608 exam contains exactly 25 questions. To pass, you must answer at least 18 correctly (72%). You cannot "average" across sections — each section requires a separate passing score.

The exam is multiple-choice with four answer options per question. There is no penalty for guessing, so answer every question. Some providers allow you to take the Core and one or more Type sections in the same sitting — others require separate appointments.

Refrigerant Rules: What EPA 608 Certification Allows You to Do

Passing the EPA 608 exam grants you legal authority to:

  • Purchase refrigerant in containers larger than 2 lbs from authorized distributors
  • Purchase, install, and operate EPA-approved refrigerant recovery equipment
  • Service, repair, and maintain refrigerant-containing equipment within your certified categories
  • Sign manifests and shipping documents for refrigerant containers

Certification does not override state contractor licensing requirements. Many states require a separate HVAC contractor license to operate a business or pull permits — check your state's licensing board for additional requirements on top of the federal EPA 608 credential.

Venting Is a Federal Crime: Intentionally releasing refrigerants into the atmosphere is prohibited under Section 608(c) of the Clean Air Act. Fines start at $44,539 per day per violation. Certification does not protect you from enforcement — it identifies you as a trained handler who is held to a higher standard of compliance.

2026 Updates: The AIM Act and HFC Phase-Down

The American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act of 2020 significantly expanded the EPA's authority over HFC refrigerants. Current EPA 608 exams now include questions on:

  • HFC phase-down schedule: The AIM Act mandates an 85% reduction in HFC production and consumption by 2036 (baseline: 2011–2013 average)
  • R-410A phase-out: R-410A, the dominant residential refrigerant, is being phased down. New residential equipment must transition to lower-GWP alternatives
  • A2L refrigerants: Next-generation refrigerants like R-32, R-454B, and R-466A are classified as A2L (mildly flammable). Technicians must understand flammability classifications, ASHRAE 34 safety groups, and updated handling/ventilation requirements for A2L systems
  • Leak rate rules: The AIM Act tightened leak rate requirements and expanded record-keeping obligations for commercial systems

What Happens After You Pass? (Certification Card and Records)

After passing the EPA 608 exam:

  • Digital certificate: Most online providers issue a digital certificate immediately upon passing. Download and save this in multiple locations.
  • Physical wallet card: Typically mailed within 2–4 weeks. The card includes your name, certification type, certification number, and the certifying organization's information.
  • Employer verification: Employers and refrigerant distributors may ask for your certification number and the issuing organization's name. Your certifying organization maintains records on file and can verify your credential by phone or online lookup.
  • No renewal required: Unlike many professional credentials, EPA 608 certification does not require continuing education or renewal exams. Your credential remains valid indefinitely under current EPA rules.

Frequently Asked Questions — EPA 608 Practice Test

What is EPA 608 certification?

EPA 608 certification is a mandatory federal credential under the Clean Air Act for any technician who purchases, handles, or recovers refrigerants in stationary HVAC/R equipment. It is administered by EPA-approved third-party testing organizations and is valid for life.

Does EPA 608 certification expire?

No. Once earned, your Section 608 certification is valid for life. There is no renewal requirement under current EPA regulations. For details on lost cards, revocation scenarios, and record verification, see the EPA 608 certification validity rules guide.

What is the passing score for the EPA 608 exam?

You must answer at least 18 out of 25 questions correctly (72%) in each section. Each section must be passed independently — you cannot average scores across sections.

How much does the EPA 608 exam cost?

Fees range from $0 to $150 depending on the provider. SkillCat often offers subsidized or free first attempts online. Mainstream Engineering charges $50–$80 for online proctoring. In-person testing at supply houses or trade schools typically runs $80–$130.

Can I buy refrigerant without EPA 608 certification?

No. Federal law prohibits purchasing refrigerant in containers larger than 2 lbs without valid Section 608 certification. Distributors are legally required to verify credentials before completing the sale.

What's the difference between Type I, II, III, and Universal?

Type I = small appliances (5 lbs or less). Type II = high-pressure systems (residential AC, heat pumps). Type III = low-pressure systems (large centrifugal chillers). Universal = all three types combined, qualifying you for any refrigerant system.

How hard is the EPA 608 exam?

The Core section is rated most difficult due to regulatory and chemistry content. Type II is the most technical of the Type sections. Most technicians who use practice tests consistently and study for 2–3 weeks pass on their first attempt. See our how to study guide for a proven approach.
