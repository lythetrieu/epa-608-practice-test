## CLEAN AIR ACT & EPA AUTHORITY
- R-400 series refrigerants can leak from a system at uneven rates due to different vapor pressures, which can affect the percentage of each refrigerant remaining.
- Fractionation is refrigerant leaking at uneven rates due to different vapor pressures.
- EPA defines four liquid-phase pressure ranges at 104 degrees F condensing temperature.
- Section 608 of the Clean Air Act sets forth the rules and regulations for refrigerant handling in the HVACR industry.
- Section 608 certification allows a technician to purchase or handle regulated refrigerants.
- Distributors that sell HCFC, HFC, or another regulated refrigerant must verify the purchaser is, or employs, a Section 608 certified technician.
- A technician must have their Section 608 certification card in their possession when performing an activity regulated by Section 608.
- If a technician's Section 608 certification card is lost, a replacement can be requested from their certifying organization, not the EPA.
- A person is not required to be Section 608 certified when servicing an external electric circuit or sections of the system that do not involve the refrigerant components of an HVACR appliance.
- The Clean Air Act allows for releasing an exempt refrigerant (e.g., ammonia and CO2).
- A small amount of refrigerant mixed with nitrogen (a leak trace gas) may be released for leak detection.
- As of 2017, a technician can be fined $44,539 per day, per violation, for violating the Clean Air Act.
- Service technicians who violate Clean Air Act provisions may lose their certification in addition to paying fines and being required to appear in court.
- State and local governments may establish laws that contain stricter requirements than the Clean Air Act/EPA regulations.
- The EPA's Significant New Alternatives Policy (SNAP) Program identifies refrigerants with lower overall risks to human health and the environment.
- Desiccant dehumidifiers are NOT covered by EPA Section 608 regulations as they do not contain a refrigerant.
- Some natural refrigerants, such as carbon dioxide, can be purchased by someone who is not Section 608 certified.
- Under Section 608 "Safe Disposal Requirements," it is the final person in the disposal chain that is responsible for ensuring refrigerant has been removed from appliances before disposal.
- Releasing mixtures of nitrogen and refrigerant from adding nitrogen to a fully charged appliance to leak check is a violation of the venting prohibition.
- Only a few ounces of refrigerant mixed with nitrogen is considered a leak trace gas and may be released without recovery.
- Leak detection methods and devices used must be approved by the manufacturer.
- An electronic or ultrasonic leak detector is the most effective method to determine the general area of a small leak.
- Newly installed systems should be pressurized with dry-nitrogen for leak detection to cause the least amount of damage to the environment.
- If an electronic leak detector is to be used, a trace of the system refrigerant can be added to the system along with the dry-nitrogen.
- A leak trace gas is not considered a refrigerant under the EPA's refrigerant management regulations.
- Once the general area of the leak is located, soap bubbles will aid in pinpointing the leak.
- Finding and repairing leaks in a system will conserve refrigerant for future use.
- Upon completion of a leak repair and before recharging: install a new filter drier and complete a standing-pressure leak check at the maximum system pressure.
- If the pressure steadily rises, there is probably a leak.
- New SNAP regulations have approved R-290, R-450A, and R-600a refrigerants for newly manufactured household refrigerators.
- When servicing a small appliance found to be leaking, it is not mandatory to repair the leak, but it is recommended.
- When installing any type of access fitting onto a sealed system, the fitting should be leak tested before proceeding with recovery.
- Solderless type piercing valves tend to leak over time and should not remain on refrigeration systems after completion of repair.
- When using nitrogen to service, leak test, or purge a refrigeration system, the nitrogen tank should be equipped with a regulator.
- Air-conditioning appliances used to supply cooling to control heat and/or humidity in occupied facilities is defined by EPA as Comfort Cooling for the leak repair category.
- When an appliance has dual use, the applicable maximum leak rate is determined by the highest percentage of use.
- If a company uses 55% of its cooling capacity for manufacturing/refrigeration and 45% for air conditioning office spaces, it is considered Industrial Process Refrigeration (IPR) for the leak rate category.
- The charge stated on the equipment nameplate is used to determine normal charge for leak rate calculations when the system is a packaged system charged at the factory.
- The leak rate of an appliance can be calculated when topping off or recharging a system due to a loss of charge.
- EPA's leak repair requirements apply to all appliances using a regulated refrigerant.
- Once the threshold leak rate has been exceeded on any system with a charge size of 50 pounds or greater, the owner must repair the appliance to bring the leak rate below the threshold.
- If the owner cannot or chooses not to bring the leak rate below the threshold, the appliance must be retrofitted or retired.
- Leak repair requirements are the same for Type II and Type III.
- As of January 1, 2019, the maximum leak rate for comfort cooling is 10% of the charge per year.
- As of January 1, 2019, the maximum leak rate for commercial refrigeration is 20% of the charge per year.
- As of January 1, 2019, the maximum leak rate for industrial process refrigeration is 30% of the charge per year.
- If an appliance with 50 or more pounds of a regulated refrigerant has exceeded the threshold leak rate, the owner/operator has 30 days to repair and conduct the initial verification test.
- If an appliance with a charge of 200 pounds or more passes the initial verification leak test, a follow-up verification test must be conducted within 10 days.
- Not having a certified service technician available cannot be used as a reason to extend the appliance leak repair deadline.
- A cold storage warehouse operating an appliance leaking above the threshold rate can continue to operate without repair for 12 months before it must be retrofitted or retired (assuming no extensions).
- If that system leak rate exceeds 125% of its full charge in a calendar year, a report must be submitted to EPA describing efforts to identify and repair leaks.
- The owner/operator has 18 months to retrofit or retire a leaking appliance if the replacement will use a refrigerant exempt from the venting prohibition.
- Leak inspections are required for appliances that have exceeded the maximum allowable leak rate.
- All visible and accessible components of an appliance must be inspected for leaks.
- Commercial refrigeration and IPR with more than 500 pounds: leak inspections required once every three months until leak rate has not exceeded 20% (commercial) or 30% (IPR) for four quarters in a row.
- Commercial refrigeration and IPR with 50 to 500 pounds: leak inspections required once per calendar year until leak rate has not exceeded 20% (commercial) or 30% (IPR) for one year.
- Comfort cooling with 50 or more pounds: leak inspections required once per calendar year until leak rate has not exceeded 10% for one year.
- Technicians are required to provide service invoices and records of leak inspections or verification tests to owners/operators.

- When absolutely necessary, dry-nitrogen with a trace amount of the system's design refrigerant should be used for leak detection.

- To pinpoint refrigerant leaks, testing with soap bubbles is best.
- A refrigerant leak in a high-pressure capillary tube system should be suspected if the system operates with excessive superheat.
- If leaks make recovery to the prescribed level unattainable, the system refrigerant only needs to be recovered until internal pressure reaches atmospheric pressure.
- Compressor replacement, as well as replacement of an evaporator coil, condenser coil, or other system heat exchanger, is considered a "major" repair as defined by EPA regulations.

- Charged low-pressure refrigeration machines may be most efficiently leak checked by adding heat with circulated hot water or heating blankets.
- To reduce refrigerant loss from the purge unit on an R-123 chiller, always leak test and repair the chiller.
- A low-pressure system could have a leak in the condenser or chiller barrel tubes if excessive moisture continuously collects in the purge unit.
- Moisture most frequently enters the refrigerant system in low-pressure chillers through leaks from areas with gaskets or fittings.
- To check for a refrigerant leak to the water side of a chiller, place a leak detector probe in the drain valve opening of the water box with the water removed.
- Drain the water sides of the evaporator and condenser prior to recovering refrigerant from a chiller suspected of having tube leaks.
- Low-pressure systems with open-drive type compressors are particularly susceptible to leaks from the shaft seal.
- As of January 1, 2019, the maximum leak rate for comfort cooling is 10% of the charge per year (Type III).

## ENVIRONMENT & OZONE DEPLETION
- Stratospheric ozone helps form the earth's protective shield.
- The ozone layer protects the earth from ultraviolet radiation from the sun.
- Ozone depletion potential (ODP) measures the ability of a substance to destroy ozone in the stratosphere.
- Ozone depletion in the stratosphere is a global problem which affects everyone on the planet.
- One of the most serious results from thinning/damaging of the stratospheric ozone layer is an increase in the rates of skin cancer in humans.
- Another human health effect from damage to the stratospheric ozone layer is the increase in cases of cataracts.
- Stratospheric ozone depletion can cause decreased crop yields and damage to marine organisms.
- Air samples taken over erupting volcanoes show that volcanoes add only small quantities of chlorine to the atmosphere compared to chlorine-containing refrigerants.
- The rise in chlorine measured in the stratosphere over the past four decades matches the rise in the amount of Fluorine over the same period.
- The rise in chlorine measured by NASA in the stratosphere over the past twenty years matches the rise in CFC and HCFC emissions over the same period.
- Ozone in the stratosphere consists of molecules containing 3 oxygen atoms (O3).
- Chlorine is the element of a CFC or HCFC molecule that causes ozone depletion.
- When a chlorine atom encounters an ozone molecule, it takes one oxygen atom, forming chlorine monoxide (ClO), and leaves an O2 molecule behind.
- The chlorine monoxide collides with another ozone molecule, releasing its oxygen atom, forming two O2 molecules, leaving the chlorine free to attack another ozone molecule.
- A single chlorine atom can last in the stratosphere for 120 years destroying up to 100,000 ozone molecules.
- The chlorine in CFCs and HCFCs will neither dissolve in water nor break down into compounds that dissolve in water, so they do not rain out of the atmosphere.
- HFCs are made up of Hydrogen, Fluorine, and Carbon.
- HFCs do not contain chlorine that effects the ozone layer.
- Most HFCs have a high Global Warming Potential (GWP).
- Carbon Dioxide (CO2) is used as the baseline measurement for global warming potential and has a GWP value of 1.
- Most HFC refrigerants, such as R-410A, have global warming potentials thousands of times greater compared to carbon dioxide.
- Hydrocarbon (HC) and Hydrofluoroolefin (HFO) refrigerants have the lowest global warming potential compared to CFCs, HCFCs, and HFCs.
- The GWP of isobutane (R-600a), propane (R-290), and R-441A are significantly lower compared to HFCs such as R-134a, R-407C, R-404A, and R-410A.
- HFO refrigerants are non-ozone depleting and have very low global warming potential.
- Most HFCs have very high global warming potentials, making them damaging to the environment.
- CFC refrigerants have the highest ozone depletion potential (ODP) and are the most harmful to stratospheric ozone.
- Chlorine is the component of CFCs that poses a threat to the stratospheric ozone.
- The hydrogen in HCFCs makes them a little less harmful to the stratospheric ozone when compared to CFCs.
- HCFCs have an ozone depletion potential of more than zero, ranging between zero and one.
- HFCs have the lowest/no ozone depletion potential but do have an effect on global warming.
- HFOs have no ozone depletion potential but do have a very small effect on global warming.
- HCs have no ozone depletion potential and a very small effect on global warming, with GWP values less than 10.
- The United States is part of the Montreal Protocol, an international treaty that addresses ozone-depleting substances and their alternatives.
- An appliance using an ozone-depleting refrigerant does not need to be removed or stored in a warehouse to extend the repair deadline.
- If a chiller with a charge of 200 pounds of an ozone-depleting refrigerant passes the initial verification leak test, a follow-up verification test must be conducted within 10 days.

## EVACUATION PROCEDURES & VACUUM
- Refrigerants emitted when cutting a line without properly evacuating the appliance violates the venting prohibition.
- The reason for dehydrating a refrigeration system is to remove water and water vapor.
- To dehydrate a system, it should be evacuated.
- It is not possible to over-evacuate a system.
- Most manufacturers require system evacuation to 500 microns or lower.
- Evacuation to 500 microns can be measured with a micron gauge.
- Never evacuate a system to the ambient air without first following proper recovery procedures and attaining the mandated recovery level.
- Factors that affect the speed and efficiency of evacuation: size of equipment being evacuated, ambient temperature, amount of moisture in the system, size of the vacuum pump and vacuum lines.
- Vacuum lines (hoses) should be equal to or larger than the pump intake connection.
- The brand of a micron gauge does not usually affect the speed of evacuation, but the method used to connect it to the system may.
- The piping connection to the vacuum pump should be as short in length as possible and as large in diameter as possible.
- For accurate readings during evacuation, the system vacuum gauge should be connected as far away from the vacuum pump as possible.
- Measuring a final system vacuum should be done with the system isolated and the vacuum pump turned off.
- A system that will not hold a vacuum after evacuation probably has a leak.
- During evacuation, heating the refrigeration system will decrease dehydration time.
- Dehydration is complete when the vacuum gauge shows the required vacuum of 500 microns or less has been reached and held.
- Before transferring refrigerant to an empty cylinder, the cylinder should be evacuated to reduce pressure and remove non-condensables.
- Hermetic compressor motors on recycling/recovery machines rely on the flow of refrigerant through the compressor for cooling and may overheat when drawing deep vacuums.
- The motor winding of a hermetic refrigeration compressor could be damaged if energized under a deep vacuum.
- Required evacuation for very high-pressure appliance: 0 inches Hg.
- Required evacuation for high-pressure appliance with less than 200 lbs. of refrigerant: 0 inches Hg.
- Required evacuation for high-pressure appliance with 200 lbs. or more of refrigerant: 10 inches Hg.
- Required evacuation for medium-pressure appliance with less than 200 lbs. of refrigerant: 10 inches Hg.
- Required evacuation for medium-pressure appliance with 200 lbs. or more of refrigerant: 15 inches Hg.
- Before making a major repair to an R-410A (high-pressure) system containing more than 200 pounds, evacuate to at least 10 inches of Hg vacuum.
- After reaching the required recovery vacuum level, wait a few minutes to see if pressure rises, indicating moisture or a leak.
- When changing out the compressor of a system containing 40 pounds of R-404A (high-pressure), evacuate the isolated section to 0 psig.
- A deep vacuum is measured in microns.
- A vacuum level of 500 microns should be achieved to ensure all moisture and non-condensables have been removed from a refrigeration system.
- When evacuating a system with large amounts of moisture, it may be necessary to break the vacuum by increasing pressure with nitrogen to counteract freezing.
- Water in the system can freeze when using too large of a vacuum pump to evacuate.
- In an R-410A chilled-water system, vapor is charged from a vacuum level to approximately 110 psig, corresponding to 36 degrees F, to prevent water freezing.
- For R-123 (a low-pressure refrigerant), the saturation pressure at 35 degrees F is 19.5 inches Hg vacuum.
- A low-pressure system operates below atmospheric pressure (in a vacuum).
- According to ASHRAE Guideline 3, the system should be checked for leaks if, during a standard vacuum test, pressure rises from 1 mm Hg to a level above 2.5 mm Hg.
- The high-pressure cut-out control on a recovery unit used for evacuating/recovering from a low-pressure chiller is typically set for 10 psig.
- A severely oversized vacuum pump could cause trapped water to freeze when evacuating/dehydrating.
- During evacuation/dehydration of a system with large amounts of moisture, it may be necessary to break the vacuum using nitrogen.
- Increasing pressure with nitrogen prevents moisture from freezing and increases the speed of dehydration.
- Water is circulated through a chiller during refrigerant evacuation/recovery to prevent freezing of water in the appliance.
- After reaching the required recovery vacuum on a low-pressure appliance, wait a few minutes to see if system pressure rises before charging.
- When leaks make evacuation/recovery to the prescribed level unattainable, do not proceed with the recovery.
- When making a non-major repair, warming the liquid refrigerant with hot water or warming blankets will bring the system pressure out of a vacuum.
- Before disposing of a low-pressure appliance, refrigerant must be recovered or evacuated to 25 mm of Hg absolute.
- Before evacuation/recovery or dehydration, an oil sample should be taken if the unit has had a compressor burn-out.
- When evacuating/dehydrating a system with large amounts of moisture, triple evacuate/dehydrate the system using dry-nitrogen to break the vacuum.
- After servicing and evacuating/dehydrating a chiller, refrigerant vapor is re-introduced to a pressure which corresponds to its saturation temperature above freezing.
- Charging liquid R-245fa into a low-pressure system in a vacuum greater than 18 inches Hg vacuum will cause the system water to freeze.
- At 18 inches Hg vacuum, R-245fa has a saturation temperature well below -40 degrees F.
- An evacuated/dehydrated water-cooled chiller system with R-123 should have a vapor pressure above 20 inches Hg vacuum before adding liquid refrigerant.
- At 35 degrees F, R-123 pressure is approximately -19.5 inches Hg vacuum (confirming the 19.5" Hg vacuum stated in Type II).
- At -40 degrees F, R-410A dew/vapor pressure is approximately -9 psig (likely vacuum reading at this temperature).
- At -40 degrees F, R-134a pressure is approximately -11.0 psig (vacuum).


## RECOVERY EQUIPMENT & PROCEDURES
- Recovery equipment must be tested according to AHRI Standard 740.
- Self-contained recovery devices remove refrigerant from an appliance without the assistance of components in the appliance.
- Recovery equipment that relies on the compressor in the appliance and/or the pressure of the refrigerant in the appliance is considered system-dependent.
- Venting refrigerant from a recovery machine or recovery cylinder after use is also illegal.
- Recover means to remove refrigerant in any condition from a system and store it in an approved recovery cylinder or container.
- Refrigerant recovery and/or recycling equipment manufactured after November 15, 1993 must be certified and labeled by an EPA-approved equipment testing organization.
- An EPA-approved certification label is required on all new recovery equipment.
- When handling and filling refrigerant cylinders or operating recovery/recycling equipment, wear safety glasses and protective gloves and follow all manufacturer's safety precautions.
- A refrigerant recovery cylinder should be free of rust, damage, be labeled, secure, and filled to no more than 80% of its capacity by weight.
- Approved refrigerant recovery cylinders can be identified by having yellow colored tops and gray bodies.
- The fill level of the recovery cylinder can be controlled by a mechanical float device, electronic shut-off device, or measuring the gross cylinder weight.
- The pitch of the recovery device does not control the fill level of the recovery cylinder.
- A domestic refrigerator is an example of an appliance on which it would be permissible to use a passive recovery device.
- Maintenance practices for recovery equipment should include checking for refrigerant leaks and oil levels on a regular basis.
- A refrigerant scale or tank internal float device are acceptable methods for monitoring that the fill level in a recovery tank does not exceed 80%.
- A vacuum pump made for system dehydration may be used to evacuate a recovery cylinder to be used as a recovery device.
- A vacuum pump cannot be used in combination with a pressurized recovery cylinder or vessel for recovery.
- It is unacceptable to mix any refrigerants in an appliance or recovery cylinder.
- When checking for non-condensables inside a recovery cylinder containing one refrigerant, the technician must allow the temperature of the cylinder to stabilize to room temperature before taking a pressure reading.
- If a technician suspects refrigerant in a recovery cylinder is contaminated, a pressure measurement should be taken and compared to a PT chart.
- When using passive recovery devices on small appliances with non-operating compressors, it is essential to take measures to help release trapped regulated refrigerant from the compressor oil.
- When using a self-contained (active) recovery device with a large volume of non-condensable gas, excessive pressure can occur on the high side of the recovery machine and in the recovery cylinder.
- Those who service Type II stationary refrigeration appliances must own active recycling and recovery equipment.
- It is no longer required that the purchase of recovery equipment be reported to the EPA.
- Cooling the recovery cylinder will lower its pressure and speed up the recovery process.
- These evacuation levels apply to recovery/recycling equipment manufactured or imported after November 15, 1993.
- Recovery and recycling equipment must be labeled as certified to meet EPA's requirements.


## RECOVERY, RECYCLING & RECLAMATION
- Recovered refrigerant may be charged into the same system, another system with the same ownership, or shipped to an approved reclaimer.

## REFRIGERANT SUBSTANCES & SUBSTITUTES
- Refrigerants may be identified by type (CFC, HCFC, HFC, HFO, HC) and ASHRAE number or with "R" plus the ASHRAE number (e.g., HFC-134a or R-134a).
- ASHRAE has grouped refrigerants by Class A (safest) or B, depending on their toxicity level to humans.
- CFC refrigerants contain chlorine, fluorine, and carbon.
- Examples of CFC refrigerants include R-11 and R-12.
- HCFC refrigerants contain hydrogen, chlorine, fluorine, and carbon.
- R-22 and R-123 are examples of HCFC refrigerants.
- HFC refrigerants contain hydrogen, fluorine, and carbon atoms connected by single bonds between the atoms.
- HFC refrigerants do not contain harmful chlorine.
- Examples of HFCs are R-134a, R-410A, R-404A, R-407C, and R-422B.
- HFO refrigerants contain hydrogen, fluorine, and carbon atoms connected with at least one double bond between the carbon atoms.
- HFOs are less flammable than hydrocarbon refrigerants but most are still mildly flammable so are classified as A2L.
- HFO refrigerants contain fluorine, making them less flammable than hydrocarbon refrigerants.
- R-1234yf is an HFO refrigerant used in chillers.
- Hydrocarbon (HC) refrigerants are an elementary compound of hydrogen and carbon.
- Due to flammability of some HCs and HFOs, equipment must be designed to handle the refrigerant.
- EPA regulates the maximum amount of refrigerant that can be used in new systems by their type and use.
- Examples of low-pressure refrigerants: CFC-11, HCFC-123, HFC-245fa, HFO-1233zd.
- Examples of medium-pressure refrigerants: CFC-12, HCFC-124, HCFC-409A, HFC-134a, HC-600a, HFO-1234yf, HFO-1234ze.
- Examples of high-pressure refrigerants: HCFC-22, HFC-404A, HFC-407C, HFC-422B, HFC-422D, HFC-410A, HC-441A, R-717.
- The U.S. is phasing out or has already phased out CFC, HCFC, and other refrigerants harmful to the environment.
- Anyone performing any activity that violates the refrigerant circuit, including removal of refrigerant or replacing a component containing a regulated refrigerant, must be certified at the proper level and have the required type of recovery equipment.
- It is illegal to knowingly release CFC, HCFC, or HFC refrigerants during the service, maintenance, repair, or disposal of appliances.
- The phase-out for the production and imports of newly manufactured R-22 and HCFC-142b in the U.S. will be in the year 2020.
- After the phase-out of all HCFC refrigerants in 2030, supplies for equipment servicing will come from recovered and reclaimed gas.
- It is not a violation to service a system that uses CFCs or HCFCs after the phase-out of those refrigerants.
- Technicians can only charge used CFC, HCFC, or HFC refrigerants back into the same appliance or into another appliance owned by the same owner.
- All CFC, HCFC, HFC, and HFO refrigerant recovery equipment must meet EPA standards.
- Recovery machines may be able to recover one or multiple types of refrigerants.
- Records that must be kept for disposed appliances include: type of refrigerant recovered, quantity of refrigerant by type, quantity recovered from disposed appliances in each calendar month, quantity of refrigerant by type sent for reclamation or destruction.
- Topping off one type of refrigerant with another can void equipment warranty, ruin the refrigerant, damage the equipment, and cause severe personal injury or death.
- When discarding disposable cylinders of CFCs, HCFCs, HFCs, or HFOs: recover all remaining refrigerant, render the cylinder useless, and recycle the metal.
- Before opening or disposing of any appliance containing CFC, HCFC, or HFC refrigerant, recover the refrigerant to approved EPA recovery or evacuation levels.
- The knowingly release of a CFC, HCFC, HFC, or HFO refrigerant during service, maintenance, repair, vandalism, theft, or disposal of appliances is illegal.
- The technician must have a separate refrigerant recovery cylinder for each type of refrigerant recovered.
- If servicing systems that use HFC-134a, HFC-410A, and HCFC-22, then three cylinders are required even though two are HFCs.
- The wrong type of electronic leak detector used with a flammable refrigerant can cause an explosion and bodily injuries.
- Any system designed to contain a flammable HC or HFO refrigerant must have a leak test before evacuating to 500 microns or lower.
- Unlike natural gas, flammable hydrocarbon or HFO refrigerants do not contain odorants to indicate their presence.
- Silicone elastomers, used in seals and gaskets, are not compatible with HFO refrigerants.
- According to ASHRAE refrigerant safety classification standard, A1 designations would be the safest.
- HFO-1234yf is classified A2L, not A3 (higher flammability).
- When transporting cylinders containing used refrigerant, a DOT classification tag and label must be attached.
- Portable, refillable tanks or containers used to ship CFC, HCFC, or other refrigerants obtained with recovery equipment must meet DOT standards.
- Before shipping any used refrigerant, a refrigerant label to identify the type of refrigerant recovered is placed on the recovery cylinder.
- Type I certification is for handling refrigerant during maintenance, service, or repair of small appliances.
- The sale of CFC, HCFC, HFC, and HFO refrigerants to service or install refrigeration and air-conditioning equipment is restricted to EPA certified technicians.
- MVAC-like systems do not meet the criteria for Type I appliance certification no matter the type or quantity of refrigerant.
- HCFCs and HFCs such as R-404A cannot be used for new Type I appliances.
- Recovery equipment used for small appliances containing CFCs, HCFCs, and HFCs must be certified by an EPA-approved laboratory.
- Persons servicing Type I appliances containing CFC, HCFC, or HFC can use either self-contained (active) or passive recovery equipment.

- With an inoperative compressor, recovery equipment for CFC, HCFC, and HFC refrigerants from small appliances for disposal must recover 80% of the refrigerant or achieve 4 inches of vacuum.
- Refrigerant recovery devices for CFCs, HCFCs, HFCs, and HFOs can be either manually closed or automatically closed when disconnected to prevent loss of refrigerant from hoses.
- Hydrocarbon (HC) R-600a is an approved refrigerant for new household refrigerators, freezers, and combination refrigeration/freezers.
- Hydrocarbon refrigerants are not approved for retrofit into existing household refrigerators designed for refrigerants other than hydrocarbons.
- Before beginning a refrigerant recovery procedure, it is always necessary to know the type of refrigerant in the system to prevent mixing in the recovery cylinder.
- To ensure all CFC, HCFC, or HFC refrigerant has been removed from a frost-free refrigerator, the defrost heater can be energized to increase the refrigerant's temperature and vaporize any liquid.
- At high temperatures, CFC and HCFC refrigerants can decompose into phosgene gas, hydrochloric, and/or hydrofluoric acids.
- The unit nameplate is the easiest way to check the type of refrigerant used in a packaged rooftop unit.
- If an HCFC rack refrigeration system in a supermarket fails the initial leak verification test, the technician has a 30-day window to make additional repairs or develop a retrofit plan.
- When recovering a different refrigerant type than the last recovered, remove any refrigerant remaining in the recovery unit and change the unit's filter.
- The owner/operator has 18 months to retrofit or retire an appliance if the replacement will use a refrigerant exempt from the venting prohibition (Type III).
- If an appliance with 50 or more pounds of regulated refrigerant has exceeded the threshold leak rate, the owner/operator has 30 days to repair and conduct the initial verification test (Type III).
- R-123 refrigerant is classified as B1 (non-flammable, high toxicity) under Standard 34 code group.
- R-1233zd refrigerant is classified as A1 (non-flammable, low-toxicity) under Standard 34 code group.

## REGULATIONS & COMPLIANCE
- ASHRAE stands for American Society of Heating Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers.
- Flammability is indicated by 1 (no flammability), 2 (low flammability), or 3 (high flammability).
- HC refrigerants are flammable but pose the least amount of danger to the environment.
- Isobutane (R-600a) and Propane (R-290) are examples of HCs.
- Some HC refrigerants are considered natural refrigerants.
- Azeotropic refrigerant mixtures contain two or more refrigerants.
- At a certain pressure, an azeotropic mixture evaporates and condenses at a constant temperature.
- Azeotropic mixtures act as a pure compound over their entire range.
- Zeotropic (non-azeotropic) refrigerant mixtures are mixtures of components that have different boiling points.
- Zeotropic blends may be binary (two-part) or ternary (three-part).
- Blended refrigerants are normally associated with "temperature glide."
- Temperature glide is due to the blended parts having different pressures for the same saturation temperature.
- Temperature glide can range a few tenths of a degree to 12 degrees or more.
- Bubble point (liquid) values are used when charging by condenser subcooling.
- Dew point (vapor) values are used for charging by suction or evaporator superheat.
- The R-400 series of refrigerants are near azeotropic blends.
- The proper charging method for R-400 series blended refrigerants is to weigh the refrigerant into the high side of the system as a liquid.
- When adding refrigerant to an undercharged system, liquid refrigerant is throttled into the low side with the system operating.
- R-407C is one of the refrigerants that has a high-temperature glide and may easily experience fractionation.
- Although saturation pressure-temperature behavior of two refrigerants might be similar, the potential of the refrigerants to oxidize copper tubing must also be considered.
- When retrofitting or converting a system to use a different refrigerant, only EPA-approved substitutes can be used.
- EPA does not approve any substitute refrigerants as direct drop-in replacements to service an R-22 or any other system.
- Low-pressure refrigerants have a pressure of 30 psig or lower at a liquid-phase temperature of 104 degrees F.
- Medium-pressure refrigerants have a pressure between 30 psig and 155 psig at a liquid-phase temperature of 104 degrees F.
- High-pressure refrigerants have a pressure between 155 psig and 340 psig at a liquid-phase temperature of 104 degrees F.
- Very high-pressure refrigerants have a pressure over 340 psig at a liquid-phase temperature of 104 degrees F.
- CO2 (R-744) is an example of a very high-pressure refrigerant.
- Ester-based oils cannot be mixed with any other type of oil.
- Ester-based oils are very hygroscopic and will absorb moisture out of the air and through some containers.
- It is the technician's responsibility to comply with any future changes in the law or EPA regulations after becoming certified.
- Reclaimed refrigerant must meet AHRI Standard 700 before it can be resold under EPA's regulations.
- EPA regulations require appliances that do not have service valves to have a service aperture or process stub.
- When disposing of appliances with 5-50 pounds of refrigerant, EPA does not require technicians to record the model and serial number of the appliance.
- EPA's refrigerant management regulations exempt certain refrigerants from the venting prohibition when the EPA determines they do not pose a threat to the environment if released.
- It is lawful to service existing R-22 systems and use existing supplies of R-22.
- It is not legal to top off an R-22 charge with another refrigerant such as R-410A.
- Records of recovered refrigerant must be kept to ensure venting does not take place after recovery from an appliance.
- Small quantities of isobutane, used in household freezers, can be vented.
- Recycle means to extract refrigerant from an appliance (except MVACs) and clean the refrigerant for reuse in equipment of the same owner without meeting all requirements for reclamation.
- Recycled refrigerant is cleaned using oil separation and single or multiple passes through devices such as replaceable core filter-driers, which reduce moisture, acidity, and particulate matter.
- Recycling equipment must meet AHRI 740 Performance Rating Standard.
- Reclaim means to process refrigerant to a level equal to new (virgin) product specifications as determined by chemical analysis.
- Reclaimed refrigerants must meet AHRI 700 Standards before they can be sold.
- Reclamation is normally performed at a chemical processing company.
- System-dependent (passive) recovery captures refrigerant with the assistance of components in the appliance from which refrigerant is being recovered.
- Self-contained (active) recovery has its own means to draw the refrigerant out of the appliance.
- Recovery time will be increased if the appliance is located in low ambient temperatures or long hoses are used.
- Long and small diameter hoses between the unit and recovery machine should be avoided as they cause excessive pressure drop and increase recovery time.
- Upon completion of refrigerant liquid transfer, guard against trapping liquid refrigerant in the service hose between closed service valves.
- The trace gas should be the same as the refrigerant the appliance is going to be charged with.
- Odorants do not need to be added to systems containing flammable refrigerants.
- If moisture is allowed to remain in an operating refrigeration system, hydrochloric and hydrofluoric acids may form.
- Tapping a compressor with a rubber mallet will aid in releasing trapped refrigerant from the oil.
- If pressure increases then stops for a few minutes then rises more, there is probably moisture left in the system.
- Recovery machines must be grounded when in use, especially when recovering a flammable refrigerant.
- When pressurizing a system with nitrogen, always charge through a pressure regulator and a relief valve in the downstream line from the pressure regulator.
- When using more than one pressure relief valve, they MUST NOT be installed in series to each other, only parallel to each other.
- If corrosion build-up is found within the body of a relief valve, the valve MUST be replaced.
- At high temperatures, refrigerants decompose and form acids.
- Hydrochloric acid is formed if the refrigerant contains chlorine.
- Hydrofluoric acid is formed if the refrigerant contains fluorine.
- Refrigerant vapors or mist in high concentrations should not be inhaled; they may cause heart irregularities or unconsciousness.
- R-410A is considered non-toxic but in high concentrations can cause asphyxia.
- Prior to recovery of any flammable refrigerant, ensure that the refrigeration system and recovery unit is grounded.
- Do not cut or weld any refrigerant line while refrigerant is in the unit.
- A red color marking is required on all process tubes and other pipes through which a flammable refrigerant flows where a service connection is probable.
- The red marking must extend a minimum of one inch in both directions from such locations.
- An alcohol spray should be used to remove ice from sight glasses or viewing glasses.
- A reusable container for refrigerant under high pressure (above 15 psig) at normal ambient temperature must be hydrostatically tested every 5 years.


## SAFETY & REFRIGERANT HANDLING
- When leak checking a system, NEVER pressurize with oxygen or compressed air.
- When mixed with some refrigerants or compressor oil, oxygen or compressed air can cause an explosion.
- To determine the safe pressure for leak testing, check the equipment data plate for the maximum low-side test pressure value.
- NEVER expose refrigerants to open flames or glowing hot metal surfaces.
- Heating a refrigerant cylinder can result in an explosion and cause serious injuries.
- If oxygen is also present, it is possible to form carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, and phosgene gas.
- If an HC refrigerant is released into a space with an ignition source, an explosion can occur if concentration is above the Lower Flammability Limit (LFL) and below the Upper Flammability Limit (UFL).
- Safety Data Sheets (SDS) should be reviewed when working with any solvents, chemicals, or refrigerants.
- In most refrigerant accidents where death occurs, oxygen deprivation is the major cause.
- Never apply an open flame or live steam to a flammable refrigerant cylinder.
- Do not use oxygen to purge lines or to pressurize the machine.
- A sufficient concentration of the refrigerant in the space and an ignition source are most likely to lead to an explosion with refrigerants.
- In the event of a large release of any refrigerant in a contained area, a self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA) or leaving the area is required.
- Never heat a refrigerant storage or recovery tank with an open flame because it may decompose forming toxic material, it can result in venting from the pressure safety valve, or the tank may explode.
- Household appliances charged with HC refrigerants require permanent safety markings on or near any evaporators, exposed refrigerant tubing, the machine compartment, and exterior of the refrigerator.
- When recovering refrigerants, safe work practices include wearing safety eyewear and butyl-lined gloves when connecting/disconnecting hoses.
- Refrigerants are heavier than air and displace oxygen; a very large leak can cause suffocation.
- If a large leak of refrigerant occurs in an enclosed area and no SCBA is available, vacate and naturally ventilate the area.
- ASHRAE Standard 15-2013 requires the use of room sensors and alarms to detect refrigerant leaks in all refrigerant safety groups.
- Room sensors and alarms are required not only because of refrigerant characteristics, but because refrigerants are heavier than air and can displace oxygen.
- Gloves and safety goggles should be worn when working with liquid refrigerant for low-pressure systems.


## SHIPPING, DISPOSAL & TRANSPORT
- Propane cylinders for grilling should never be used as a refrigerant, as they contain impurities that can damage refrigeration equipment.
- Used refrigerant may no longer meet the AHRI Standard for virgin refrigerant so it cannot change ownership without being reclaimed.
- Recycled refrigerant may be charged into the same system or another system with the same ownership.
- The technician should have a separate cylinder for refrigerants known to be mixed.
- A disposable refrigerant cylinder may never be used to recover refrigerant.
- When empty, the vapor should be recovered, reducing the pressure in a disposable cylinder to 0 psig before scrapping.
- DOT regulations require that the number of cylinders of each gas be recorded on the shipping paper for hazard class 2.2 Nonflammable Compressed Gases.
- Refrigerant cylinders must be positioned upright when shipped.
- When filling a charging cylinder with a regulated refrigerant, the refrigerant vented off the top must be recovered.
- If the PT relationship does not match, the refrigerant should be turned in to a reclamation facility.
- The approximate pressure of an R-134a cylinder stored at 80 degrees F with no non-condensable impurities is 87 psig.
- As long as liquid refrigerant is present and no impurities in the cylinder, pressure and temperature should match the PT chart.
- Refrigerant recovered from an air-conditioning system and held in a refillable cylinder may be charged back into the system after repair.
- Nitrogen tanks are under high pressures, so nitrogen cylinders require a pressure regulator.


## SMALL APPLIANCE RECOVERY
- Type I technicians or Universal technicians can service small appliances, with the exception of MVAC.
- The EPA definition of a small appliance includes products manufactured, fully charged, and hermetically sealed in a factory containing five pounds or less of refrigerant.
- Split-systems may not be serviced by Type I technicians.
- When retrofitting or converting a system, only EPA-approved substitutes can be used.
- There is no such thing as a "drop-in" substitute; EPA does not approve any substitute refrigerants as a direct drop-in replacement.
- Hoses with manual or self-sealing valves on the ends should be used to minimize the release of refrigerant when attaching a gauge set.
- R-744 (carbon dioxide/CO2) is a very high-pressure refrigerant and generally does not need to be recovered.
- When refrigerant is recovered from an appliance, it should be recovered into a recovery vessel clearly marked as to its content.
- Comparisons to a pressure-temperature chart are only valid to check for contaminated refrigerant if both pressure and temperature of the refrigerant are stable and known.
- Some reclamation facilities may refuse to accept mixed refrigerants or charge extra for processing or destroying them.
- The service aperture or process stub on small appliances is a straight piece of tubing entered using a piercing access valve.
- A passive, system-dependent, recovery process for small appliances can capture refrigerant in a non-pressurized container.
- It is helpful to heat (with a heat gun) and strike the compressor with a rubber mallet when recovering refrigerants into a non-pressurized container from a small appliance with an inoperative compressor.
- Heating and striking helps free refrigerant trapped under the oil in the compressor crankcase.
- When using the system-dependent (passive) recovery process and the compressor does not run, both the high and low side of the system must be accessed for recovery.
- Both low and high-side access valves should be installed when recovering from a household refrigerator to improve the speed of recovery.
- An appliance with an operating compressor and completely restricted capillary tube or metering device requires only one access valve on the high side of the system.
- If the pressure in an appliance is 0 psig after installing and opening a piercing access valve, the recovery procedure cannot begin.
- Non-condensable gas in the refrigerant can be checked for by using a PT chart.
- During system recovery and/or repair, a pungent odor from the refrigerant normally indicates a compressor burn-out.
- The technician should watch for signs of oil contamination when recovering from a system that experienced a compressor burn-out.
- The system will have to be flushed if contaminants are present from a compressor burn-out.
- When nitrogen is used to pressurize or blow debris out of the system, the nitrogen may be vented to the ambient air.


## HIGH-PRESSURE RECOVERY & SERVICE
- Type II certification is for maintaining, servicing, repairing, or disposing of medium, high, and very high-pressure appliances, except small appliances and MVAC.
- Residential, office, and commercial buildings are included in the Comfort Cooling category.
- Appliances used to preserve products, as in the retail food and cold storage warehouse sectors, are classified by EPA as Commercial Refrigeration.
- The charge in field-piped split-systems can be calculated using the factory charge plus the charge added for piping and accessories.
- The unit's nameplate will indicate the system's total charge.
- The best method to charge a system with an air-cooled condenser and evaporator with a nameplate charge of 80 pounds is a liquid charge by weight through the liquid-line service valve.
- After exceeding 125% of full charge, further action must be taken by repairing, retiring, mothballing, or retrofitting the appliance.
- The repair deadline can be extended by temporarily shutting down the appliance, recovering refrigerant from an isolated section or the entire system to at least atmospheric pressure.
- An initial verification test must be performed before any additional refrigerant is added to the appliance.
- A follow-up verification test must be performed only after the appliance has returned to normal operating characteristics and conditions; there is no minimum timeframe.
- The owner and/or operator of the equipment is responsible for keeping the records.
- Before recovering refrigerant, determine the maximum amount of refrigerant contained in the system (total charge).
- If total charge is greater than 15 pounds, system-dependent (passive) recovery cannot be used.
- EPA regulations prohibit passive recovery when the appliance contains over 15 pounds of refrigerant.
- Before using a recovery unit, the service valve positions and recovery oil level should be checked.
- The gauge hoses used for all field service work are required to have self-sealing connectors or hand valves to minimize refrigerant release.
- Before recovering refrigerant in a parallel piped compressor system, make certain that the equalization connections between compressors are closed.
- Recovery machines that utilize a water-cooled condenser are generally connected to the municipal water supply.
- To reduce recovery time, connect one service hose to the liquid line and recover in the liquid phase or remove from the condenser outlet when the condenser is below the receiver.
- After liquid refrigerant has been recovered, the remaining vapor is removed by the recovery system.
- Recovering refrigerant in the vapor phase, although slower than liquid recovery, will minimize oil loss from the appliance.
- All HVAC service companies must keep records for three (3) years for each appliance disposed of if the system's refrigerant charge is between 5 and 50 pounds.
- Heating the system will also speed up the recovery process by increasing the pressure differential between the tank and the system.
- The refrigerant receiver is typically found in the liquid line at the outlet of the condenser when the system uses a thermostatic expansion valve.
- When the system is operating, the state of refrigerant leaving the receiver is a high-pressure liquid.
- Prior to opening a system with a liquid-line receiver for repair, the refrigerant should be pumped down and isolated in the receiver whenever possible.
- A moisture indicating sight glass is used for checking refrigerant for moisture.
- The sight glass is located in the liquid line, immediately downstream (after) the liquid-line filter drier.
- Anytime a system is opened for service, the liquid-line filter drier should be replaced.
- The filter drier is used to remove moisture from the refrigerant in a system.
- Isopropyl alcohol should be used to remove ice from sight glasses or viewing glasses.
- In the suction line, at the outlet of the evaporator, suction line accumulators can be found.
- Accumulators are located between the evaporator and the compressor.
- Accumulators prevent liquid refrigerant that may flow from the evaporator from flowing into the compressor.
- The normal position of the suction service/shutoff valve is back-seated.
- In the back-seated position, the gauge port is closed off so the manifold hose can be removed without releasing refrigerant.
- On the high side of the compressor, the discharge valve can be found.
- The normal operating position of the discharge valve is back-seated.
- Under no circumstances should the discharge service valve be closed on an operating reciprocating compressor; doing so can result in compressor damage and severe personal injury.
- Refrigerant systems must be protected by a high-pressure relief device which may be in the compressor or external relief valve.
- When using multiple pressure relief valves, they can only be installed in parallel.
- Crankcase heaters are used on compressors to reduce the amount of refrigerant in the oil and reduce oil foaming in the compressor crankcase.
- If a compressor burns out, an oil sample should be taken to test for the presence of acid in the system.
- If non-condensables such as air or dry-nitrogen remain in a refrigeration system, the discharge pressure will be high.
- On a refrigeration system, never install multiple pressure relief valves in series.
- The saturation pressure of R-410A at 80 degrees F (room temperature) while idle is 238 psig on the PT chart.
- An R-134a chiller should be initially charged with vapor to approximately 30 psig, corresponding to 35 degrees F.
- The evaporation temperature of R-134a at 0 psig is -15 degrees F.
- ASHRAE Standard 15 requires the use of sensors to detect the presence of refrigerant with few exceptions.
- ASHRAE Standard 15 does not require an equipment room refrigerant detector for R-717 provided the mechanical ventilation system in the machinery room is run continuously.


## LOW-PRESSURE CHILLER RECOVERY
- Type III certification is for maintaining, servicing, repairing, or disposing of low-pressure appliances.
- Low-pressure chillers require purge units because they operate below atmospheric pressure.
- The purge unit gets its suction from a connection on top of the condenser where most non-condensables accumulate.
- After separating air from refrigerant, the purge unit sends the refrigerant back to the evaporator.
- High head pressure indicates air in a low-pressure system and that the purge unit may not be operating properly.
- High-efficiency purge units will discharge a low percentage of refrigerant with the air they remove.
- To prevent air accumulation into an idled low-pressure system, maintain system pressure slightly above atmospheric pressure.
- System mothballing does not require removing the appliance and storing it in a warehouse.
- Refrigerant removal from a low-pressure system starts with liquid removal followed by recovery of vapor refrigerant.
- After all liquid has been removed, an average 350-ton R-123 chiller at 0 psig would still have approximately 100 pounds of refrigerant in the vapor state.
- A water-cooled recovery unit will allow for faster recovery of large quantities of refrigerant.
- The system's water pumps, recovery compressor, and recovery condenser water should all be on during vapor or liquid removal from a low-pressure system.
- Raising the temperature of the system or room in which a low-pressure chiller is located will result in faster refrigerant recovery.
- When removing refrigerant oil, it should be heated to 130 degrees F as less refrigerant will be contained in the oil at the higher temperature.
- The rupture disc on a recovery vessel for low-pressure refrigerants relieves at 15 psig.
- Replacing or repairing a compressor, condenser, or evaporator is considered a major repair (Type III).
- Recovery records for disposed appliances with 5-50 lbs. of refrigerant must be kept for three (3) years.
- Introducing vapor first prevents liquid refrigerant charged into the system from freezing water in the heat exchanger tube bundle.
- The liquid refrigerant is charged into the system using the evaporator charging valve, which is the lowest access point on a low-pressure appliance.
- To convert from absolute pressure (psia) to gauge pressure (psig), subtract 14.7 from the absolute pressure.
- 14.7 psia equals 0 psig.
- When the TLV-TWA (Threshold Limit Value - Time Weighted Average) is exceeded, ASHRAE Standard 15-2013 requires that each machinery room activate an alarm and a mechanical ventilation system.
- The discharge from a rupture disc or pressure relief valves should be piped outdoors to prevent venting into the machine room.
- All pressure relief valves should be piped or installed in parallel, never in series (Type III).
- A 15 psig rated rupture disc is located on the evaporator of a centrifugal chiller.
- When charging through the evaporator charging valve, do not over-pressurize the system.
- Isopropyl alcohol should be used to remove ice from sight glasses or viewing glasses (Type III).
