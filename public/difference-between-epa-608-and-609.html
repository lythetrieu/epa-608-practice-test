EPA 608 vs 609: Side-by-Side Comparison of Both Certifications

Two separate federal certifications. Different equipment. Different exams. Neither substitutes for the other.

EPA 608 and EPA 609 are two separate federal certification requirements under the Clean Air Act. They cover different equipment categories, administered under different subparts of 40 CFR Part 82, and one does not substitute for the other. A technician with Universal 608 certification who services a car's air conditioning system without 609 certification is in violation of federal law. This comparison maps both certifications side by side — what each covers, which exam each requires, and which certification applies to your work.

EPA 608 Coverage: Stationary Equipment Under Section 608

Regulation Section 608, Clean Air Act Section 609, Clean Air Act

Substitutes for the other? NO NO

EPA 608 covers:

  • Commercial and residential HVAC systems (rooftop units, split systems, package units)
  • Commercial refrigeration (walk-in coolers, display cases, reach-in units)
  • Industrial process refrigeration
  • Low-pressure centrifugal chillers
  • Any stationary appliance using refrigerants covered under Section 608

EPA 608 does NOT cover:

  • Motor vehicle air conditioning (MVAC) — cars, light trucks, SUVs under 8,500 lbs GVWR
  • Vehicles over 8,500 lbs GVWR may fall under EPA 608 depending on equipment configuration

EPA 609 Coverage: Motor Vehicle AC Under Section 609

EPA 609 covers:

  • Motor vehicle air conditioning (MVAC) systems in vehicles under 8,500 lbs GVWR
  • Passenger cars, light trucks, SUVs, passenger vans in the same weight class

EPA 609 certification structure:

  • Technician certification: Individual technicians who service MVAC systems
  • Equipment certification: The recovery/recycling equipment used must be certified separately
  • Open-book format: The technician certification exam may be taken open-book

Refrigerant in MVAC systems: Modern vehicles primarily use R-134a (being phased to R-1234yf under the AIM Act). Both refrigerants fall under Section 609's MVAC refrigerant requirements — not Section 608.

Do I Need Both EPA 608 and EPA 609?

HVAC technicians who work exclusively on stationary equipment: Need only EPA 608. EPA 609 is not required and not applicable.

Auto mechanics and automotive service technicians who work on car AC: Need EPA 609. EPA 608 is not applicable to MVAC systems and does not satisfy the 609 requirement.

Technicians who service both stationary HVAC and vehicle AC (mobile mechanic shops, fleet service): Need both EPA 608 and EPA 609. Neither substitutes for the other — the Clean Air Act regulations are specific about which certification covers which equipment category. Fleet Vehicle Edge Case

If you service a commercial vehicle's HVAC as part of a broader HVAC installation (e.g., bus or van with stationary-type equipment), confirm with your employer whether that specific equipment falls under 608 or 609. The cutoff is 8,500 lbs GVWR and the equipment type — stationary vs mobile.

Exam Differences: 608 vs 609

EPA 608 exam:

  • 4 sections: Core + Type I + Type II + Type III
  • 25 questions per section (100 total for Universal)
  • Passing threshold: 18 of 25 (72%) per section, proctored closed-book
  • Type I only may be offered open-book mail-in at 84% threshold
  • Administered by EPA-approved certifying organizations (ESCO, Mainstream Engineering, HVAC Excellence)

EPA 609 exam:

  • Single technician certification exam
  • Open-book format permitted for technician exam
  • Passing threshold: 84% (21 of 25) for open-book
  • Equipment (recovery/recycling machines) must also be certified separately by an SAE-approved organization
  • Administered by EPA-approved organizations (many auto industry organizations)

EPA 608 vs 609 FAQ

What is the difference between EPA 608 and EPA 609?
EPA 608 covers stationary refrigeration and HVAC equipment. EPA 609 covers motor vehicle air conditioning (MVAC) systems. They are separate certifications under separate regulatory subparts.
Does EPA 608 Universal certification allow me to work on car AC?
No. Universal 608 certification does not authorize MVAC service. A separate EPA 609 certification is required.
Which EPA certification do I need for commercial HVAC work?
EPA 608 — Type II or Universal. EPA 609 is irrelevant to stationary commercial HVAC equipment.
Is EPA 609 easier than EPA 608?
EPA 609 covers one equipment category (MVAC) with an open-book exam option. EPA 608 Universal covers four sections. Relative difficulty depends on background — automotive technicians find 609 easier; HVAC technicians find 608 easier.

R-134a: The Refrigerant That Can Fall Under Either Regulation

R-134a in a stationary refrigeration system (reach-in cooler, commercial display case) is covered under EPA 608. The exact same refrigerant in a car's air conditioning system is covered under EPA 609. The certification requirement follows the equipment category, not the refrigerant identity.

Penalties for Working Without the Right Certification

Both Section 608 and Section 609 are enforced under the Clean Air Act, which gives the EPA authority to impose civil penalties up to $44,539 per day per violation (adjusted periodically for inflation). Enforcement priorities include:

  • Knowingly venting regulated refrigerants to atmosphere
  • Purchasing refrigerant in containers greater than 20 lbs without certification
  • Employing uncertified technicians for refrigerant-contact work

Beyond federal penalties, state-level HVAC licensing boards can revoke or suspend licenses for Section 608 violations. Insurance carriers may also deny claims related to equipment damage caused by uncertified technician work.

Which Certification for Which Career Path

HVAC Service Technician (residential / commercial)

Need: EPA 608 — Type II or Universal. Not required: EPA 609 unless you explicitly service vehicle AC as part of your work. Most HVAC employers require Universal certification. Type II alone covers the most common residential and commercial equipment (split systems, rooftop units, package units with R-410A or R-454B).

Commercial Refrigeration Technician (walk-ins, display cases)

Need: EPA 608 — Type I, II, or Universal depending on equipment charge size. Walk-in coolers and display cases are stationary equipment under Section 608. Universal certification is standard for commercial refrigeration roles.

Auto AC Mechanic

Need: EPA 609 only (plus appropriate equipment certification). EPA 608 is irrelevant unless the shop also services stationary HVAC. Some fleet shops do both — those technicians need both 608 and 609.

Chiller Technician (large commercial, industrial)

Need: EPA 608 — Type III or Universal. Centrifugal chillers using R-123, R-11, or legacy CFCs fall under Type III (low-pressure). Universal certification covers Type III along with everything else.

General Maintenance Technician (building facilities)

Need: EPA 608 — Type II is sufficient for most facility HVAC maintenance work. Universal is recommended if the building also has refrigeration equipment. EPA 609 is only needed if the facilities role includes servicing fleet or vehicle AC.

Getting Both 608 and 609: Logistics

If your work genuinely requires both certifications, here's the most efficient path:

  1. Start with EPA 608 Universal — harder exam, more prep required, covers the most equipment. Once you have Universal, you can work on all stationary HVAC and refrigeration equipment.
  2. Add EPA 609 separately — open-book format allowed, shorter study time. Most automotive technician training programs include 609 prep. The 609 exam is typically administered by auto industry organizations (ASE-accredited programs, vehicle manufacturer training centers).

There is no official combined "608 + 609" exam. They are entirely separate credentialing systems under different subparts of 40 CFR Part 82.

AIM Act Updates: How 608 and 609 Are Evolving

The AIM Act (American Innovation and Manufacturing Act, 2020) authorized EPA to phase down high-GWP HFC refrigerants. This affects both 608 and 609 technicians through new refrigerants entering service:

  • EPA 608 technicians: A2L refrigerants (R-32, R-454B, R-466A) are entering residential and commercial HVAC rapidly. These are mildly flammable — handling procedures differ from traditional HFCs. EPA 608 certification covers these refrigerants, but technicians should seek additional A2L safety training.
  • EPA 609 technicians: R-1234yf is the approved R-134a replacement for new vehicle AC systems and is already standard in most new-model vehicles. Section 609 regulations were updated to cover R-1234yf under the same MVAC refrigerant requirements as R-134a.

Neither change affects certification expiration — both EPA 608 and EPA 609 certifications remain permanent for technicians who already hold them. New refrigerant handling knowledge is expected as professional development, but there is no formal re-certification required as new refrigerants phase in.

EPA 608 vs 609 FAQ

What is the difference between EPA 608 and EPA 609?
EPA 608 covers stationary refrigeration and HVAC equipment. EPA 609 covers motor vehicle air conditioning (MVAC) systems. They are separate certifications under separate regulatory subparts of 40 CFR Part 82.
Does EPA 608 Universal certification allow me to work on car AC?
No. Universal 608 certification does not authorize MVAC service. A separate EPA 609 certification is required for motor vehicle air conditioning systems in vehicles under 8,500 lbs GVWR.
Which EPA certification do I need for commercial HVAC work?
EPA 608 — Type II or Universal. Type II covers high-pressure stationary equipment (R-410A, R-454B, R-22 systems). Universal covers all four equipment categories. EPA 609 is not applicable to stationary commercial HVAC.
Is EPA 609 easier than EPA 608?
EPA 609 covers one equipment category (MVAC) and an open-book exam is permitted. EPA 608 Universal covers four sections with a closed-book proctored exam. Relative difficulty depends on background — automotive technicians find 609 more relevant; HVAC technicians find 608 more relevant.
Can I get EPA 608 and EPA 609 at the same time?
There is no combined exam. They are separate certifications administered by separate approved organizations. You can schedule them back-to-back but must register and test for each independently.
What is the penalty for servicing refrigerant systems without EPA 608 certification?
Civil penalties under the Clean Air Act can reach $44,539 per day per violation. Employers who knowingly employ uncertified technicians for refrigerant-contact work face the same penalty structure. State licensing boards may impose additional sanctions.
