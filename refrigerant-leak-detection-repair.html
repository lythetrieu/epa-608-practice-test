Refrigerant Leak Detection & Repair: EPA 608 Exam Guide

Leak rate thresholds, mandatory repair timelines, detection methods, and 2026 AIM Act updates — every testable fact for the Core and Type II sections.

EPA 608 Practice Test › Study Guides › Refrigerant Leak Detection & Repair

Refrigerant leak detection and repair is one of the most heavily tested topics on the EPA 608 Core and Type II exam sections. The regulations are specific and testable: exact percentage thresholds trigger mandatory repair, specific timelines govern how quickly leaks must be fixed, and the 2016 and 2021 regulatory updates expanded these rules to cover HFC refrigerants. This guide covers every testable fact in this topic area.

Leak Rate Thresholds — Memorize These
  • • 10% annual leak rate → mandatory repair for comfort cooling equipment
  • • 20% annual leak rate → mandatory repair for commercial refrigeration
  • • 30% annual leak rate → mandatory repair for industrial process refrigeration
  • • Threshold applies to: Systems with refrigerant charges ≥ 50 lbs
  • • Repair timeline: 30 days; up to 120 days for complex repairs

Leak Rate Thresholds by Equipment Type

Section 608 of the Clean Air Act requires equipment owners to repair refrigerant leaks when annual leak rates exceed specific thresholds. The threshold depends on the type of equipment:

Comfort cooling (HVAC systems in occupied buildings)10%50 lbs refrigerant30 days (120 day extension available)
Commercial refrigeration (supermarkets, food service)20%50 lbs refrigerant30 days (120 day extension available)
Industrial process refrigeration (manufacturing, chemical)30%50 lbs refrigerant120 days (180 day extension for complex)

Systems Below 50 lbs Charge — No Threshold Requirement

The mandatory leak rate thresholds apply only to systems with refrigerant charges of 50 lbs or more. Smaller systems (under 50 lbs) do not have specific leak rate thresholds under Section 608 — though the general venting prohibition still applies. Small appliances (Type I, ≤5 lbs) are excluded from leak repair requirements entirely.

Calculating the Annual Leak Rate

Annual leak rate is calculated as: (pounds of refrigerant added in a 12-month period) ÷ (full refrigerant charge of the system) × 100%.

Example: A comfort cooling system has a full charge of 100 lbs. Over 12 months, 12 lbs of refrigerant was added to maintain charge. Leak rate = 12 ÷ 100 × 100% = 12%. This exceeds the 10% threshold, triggering mandatory repair.

⚠️ Exam Tip: Threshold vs. Charge Amount

Many exam questions give you a charge amount (e.g., "a 200 lb system") and ask how much refrigerant can be lost before triggering mandatory repair. For a 200 lb comfort cooling system: 200 × 10% = 20 lbs. For a 200 lb commercial refrigeration system: 200 × 20% = 40 lbs. Practice these calculations — they appear in Type II scenario questions.

Mandatory Repair Timeline

When an annual leak rate exceeds the applicable threshold:

  1. 30-day initial repair deadline: The equipment owner must have the leak repaired within 30 days of discovery.
  2. 120-day extension (comfort cooling and commercial refrigeration): If the repair requires ordering parts or takes longer, the owner can extend to 120 days total, but must submit a written request with documentation.
  3. 120-day initial + 180-day extension (industrial process refrigeration): Under the AIM Act, industrial process refrigeration gets 120 days for initial repair and an additional 180 days for complex situations requiring equipment shutdown.
  4. Retrofit or retire: If the system cannot be economically repaired, the owner must retrofit the system to use an acceptable substitute refrigerant or retire the equipment. A written plan must be submitted to the EPA if repair within the deadline is not possible.

Refrigerant Leak Detection Methods

The EPA 608 exam tests knowledge of acceptable leak detection methods, their relative sensitivity, and their appropriate applications.

Electronic Leak Detectors

The most sensitive and widely used method. Can detect leaks as small as 0.1 oz/year for some models. Must be selected for the refrigerant family (heated diode for HFCs/HCFCs, infrared for all types, ultrasonic for gross leaks). Electronic detectors require regular calibration and sensor replacement.

Ultraviolet (UV) Dye

UV fluorescent dye is injected into the system and circulates with the refrigerant. Leaks are detected by scanning with a UV (black) light — dye fluoresces bright yellow-green at the leak point. Effective for locating the exact point of a known leak. Must use dye compatible with the refrigerant and system components.

Soap Bubble Testing

Apply soap solution to suspected leak points and look for bubbles. Effective for gross leaks only — cannot detect small leaks. Simple, inexpensive, requires no equipment. Not suitable for systems with leaks below approximately 0.5 oz/year.

Standing Pressure Test

Pressurize the system with dry nitrogen (never oxygen or compressed air) and monitor pressure over time. A pressure drop indicates a leak. Used primarily after repair to verify the repair before recharging. Do not use refrigerant for pressure testing — refrigerant leaks during testing violate Section 608.

Halide Torch

An older method using an open flame that changes color in the presence of halogenated refrigerants. Still appears on exam questions for historical context. The reaction produces phosgene gas (highly toxic) — this method is rarely used in modern HVAC practice and is not recommended. Know that halide torches detect halogenated refrigerants and produce a toxic reaction product.

A2L Refrigerant Leak Detection (2026 Requirement)

The transition to A2L refrigerants (R-454B, R-32) introduces new leak detection requirements:

  • Automatic leak detection systems required for certain A2L applications under the 2021 International Mechanical Code (IMC) and International Residential Code (IRC) where charge sizes exceed specific limits
  • A2L leak detection sensors must be positioned at floor level (A2L refrigerants are denser than air and will accumulate at the lowest point)
  • Detection thresholds are set at percentages of the Lower Flammability Limit (LFL) — typically 25% LFL for warning alarms
  • A2L-certified electronic leak detectors differ from A1 detectors — verify the detector is rated for A2L refrigerants before use

Recordkeeping Requirements

When a system with a charge of 50 lbs or more loses refrigerant in excess of the applicable threshold, the equipment owner is required to:

  • Maintain service records showing the date and quantity of refrigerant added to the system
  • Document the calculated leak rate when it exceeds the threshold
  • Record the date the leak was identified and the repair action taken
  • Retain records for at least 3 years and make them available for EPA inspection

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the refrigerant leak rate thresholds that require mandatory repair?

For systems with 50 lbs or more of refrigerant charge: 10% annual leak rate for comfort cooling (HVAC in buildings), 20% for commercial refrigeration, and 30% for industrial process refrigeration. When these thresholds are exceeded, the equipment owner must repair the leak within 30 days (or 120 days for complex repairs requiring parts or extended work).

What is the repair timeline after detecting a refrigerant leak?

Standard: 30 days from when the leak was detected. Extension available: up to 120 days total (90-day extension) if the repair requires ordering parts or extended work, with proper documentation. For industrial process refrigeration under the AIM Act: 120 days initially, with an additional 180-day extension for complex repairs requiring equipment shutdown.

What leak detection methods are acceptable for EPA 608?

Electronic leak detectors (most sensitive), UV dye + black light, soap bubble testing (gross leaks only), standing pressure test with nitrogen (for post-repair verification). Halide torches are older and rarely used — they detect halogenated refrigerants but produce toxic phosgene gas. Electronic detectors must be calibrated for the specific refrigerant family being tested.

Do HFC refrigerants have the same leak repair requirements as CFCs?

Yes. Since 2016, EPA Section 608 leak repair requirements apply equally to HFC and HFO substitute refrigerants — not just ozone-depleting CFCs and HCFCs. R-410A, R-134a, R-404A systems with charges above 50 lbs are subject to the same 10%, 20%, and 30% thresholds and repair timelines as CFC and HCFC systems.

What happens if a leak cannot be repaired within the required timeline?

The equipment owner must either request an extension (with documentation) or submit a plan to retrofit or retire the equipment. If repair within the maximum extension period is not possible, the owner must retrofit the system to use an acceptable alternative refrigerant or retire the equipment. A written plan must be submitted to the EPA in these cases.
